# Axis Loss / CIF / TIF / SIF: Observation-Document Record Perspective Note

## 1. Purpose

This document is a note for organizing issues related to axis loss and its subordinate observation units, including CIF, TIF, and SIF, not from the standpoint of **general theories of rights** or **claims of ownership over phenomena**, but from the standpoint of **what is being recorded as an observation document**.

The focus of this document is as follows.

- The object of record is not the phenomenon itself as it appears across reality.
- The object of record is the formalized result produced by cutting that phenomenon according to specific boundaries and classification criteria, and arranging it into an observable structure.
- Axis loss is a higher-level observation coordinate, while CIF, TIF, and SIF are subordinate observation units under it.
- Therefore, this document does not presuppose any claim of ownership or monopoly over phenomena in general.
- Instead, its purpose is to clarify what layer is being recorded as an observation document, and what the core unit of that record is.

---

## 2. Basic Position

### 2.1 The phenomenon itself belongs to the domain of free observation

Phenomena referred to in relation to axis loss, CIF, TIF, SIF, and similar units may be repeatedly observed across reality.

That is:

- anyone may observe similar phenomena;
- anyone may describe them in different language; and
- anyone may hold a similar problem awareness.

This is not unusual, and it is not the core issue in itself.

Accordingly, the premise of this document is as follows.

> A phenomenon that exists in reality is not an object of ownership attributable to a specific individual.

This applies equally to axis loss as a whole and to its subordinate observation units, including CIF, TIF, and SIF.

### 2.2 The focus is not the phenomenon, but the recording method of the observation document

The issue is not **the existence of the phenomenon itself**, but how that phenomenon was cut by boundaries and recorded as a structure.

In other words, the core of an observation document lies in the following elements.

- What was treated as a single phenomenon unit?
- Under what conditions was it separated and identified?
- What was included, and what was excluded?
- What subordinate structures were connected to what higher-level structures?
- How were transitions and intersections between different phenomena recorded?
- In what unit of expression was it fixed so that it could be re-observed?

From the standpoint of this document, the core is not **phenomena in general**, but **the structuring of observation and the form of record**.

## 3. Object of Record as an Observation Document

### 3.1 The object of record is not “reality as it is”

An observation document does not reproduce reality as a whole.  
It records specific recurring patterns in reality by cutting them into **identifiable units**.

Therefore, the object of record is the result of a mixture of the following three elements.

1. A phenomenon observed in reality
2. The boundary established to distinguish that phenomenon
3. The formalized result that fixes that boundary in language and structure

In other words, an observation document is not a simple list of facts, but a **record of structural cuts made on observed phenomena**.

### 3.2 The unit of record is not a “phenomenon name,” but a “structured observation unit”

For example, when recording CIF, the core is not merely assigning a name to something.

The core is that the following units are fixed together.

- Definition
- Exclusion conditions
- Boundary conditions
- Occurrence or identification conditions
- Post-event processing path
- Differences from adjacent concepts
- Method of connection to the higher-level axis

Therefore, CIF, TIF, and SIF are not recorded as single words, but as **structured subordinate observation units**.

Meanwhile, axis loss is not a simple heading or abstract slogan. It functions as a **higher-level recording coordinate** that groups multiple subordinate observation units.

## 4. Document-Level Position of Axis Loss and Subordinate Observation Units

### 4.1 Axis loss is a higher-level observation coordinate

Axis loss is closer to a higher-level coordinate for rearranging multiple individual observation units under the frame of axis collapse or loss of judgment axis, rather than a term pointing to one individual event.

In other words, axis loss performs the following roles in the document.

- It groups subordinate units such as CIF, TIF, and SIF.
- It organizes the differences and connections among subordinate concepts.
- It explains transition points and cross-observations at a higher level.
- It allows individual cases to be read again within a higher-level structure.

Therefore, axis loss is closer to a **higher-level frame that aligns the entire observation document**, rather than a standalone phenomenon name.

### 4.2 CIF is a high-density formalized unit under axis loss

Based on the current density of formalization, CIF is one of the relatively densely recorded subordinate observation units under axis loss.

The reasons are as follows.

- Its definition and boundaries are presented relatively clearly.
- What is not CIF, and where something begins to transition into CIF, are relatively distinguishable.
- It has been formalized to a level that can be reapplied to other cases or structures.

Therefore, CIF functions not merely as a name, but as a **core subordinate structural unit** within the observation document.

### 4.3 TIF and SIF are also observation units under axis loss

TIF and SIF are also units observed under axis loss.

They are not meant to claim ownership over independent fragments of reality. Rather, they operate as subordinate units for:

- identifying specific collapse patterns or processing paths;
- distinguishing their differences from, and points of contact with, CIF; and
- recording transition points or intersection structures.

Therefore, TIF and SIF should also be understood not merely as individual names, but as **subordinate observation units functioning under the higher-level coordinate of axis loss**.

## 5. The Core of the Observation Document Is “Formalization,” Not “Invention”

### 5.1 What this document does not presuppose

This document does not presuppose the following claims.

- I was the first to make the phenomenon exist in the world.
- Only I can observe that phenomenon.
- If another person observes a similar phenomenon, they have immediately infringed my concept.
- Because I named it first, phenomena in general become mine.

Such claims do not fit the nature of an observation document.

### 5.2 What this document focuses on

The focus of the observation document is as follows.

- How was a specific phenomenon cut into an identifiable unit?
- What boundaries and exclusion criteria does that cut have?
- How was that observation unit arranged under a higher-level axis?
- How were the relationships among different observation units recorded?
- How was this fixed through document structure, tables, tags, definitions, and cross-references?

In other words, what is recorded here is not **the invention of a phenomenon**, but **a formalized observation structure for that phenomenon**.

## 6. Justification Points as an Observation Document

### 6.1 An observation document does not close phenomena in general

An observation document does not attempt to close off or monopolize all similar reality merely because it describes a specific phenomenon.

Rather, an observation document does the following.

- It captures recurring patterns that already exist in reality.
- It rewrites those patterns into distinguishable forms.
- It provides observation coordinates so that the same or similar phenomena can be seen again later.

Therefore, the function of an observation document is closer to **fixing identifiability** than to ownership.

### 6.2 The distinctiveness of the record comes from the observation structure

The distinctiveness of an observation document does not come from the phenomenon itself.  
It arises from points such as the following.

- Method of cutting
- Method of classification
- Method of higher-lower connection
- Setting of transition points
- Method of recording intersection structures
- Combination of definitions and exclusion statements
- Document-level arrangement and reference system

In other words, the core of distinctiveness lies less in **what was seen** than in **how it was structured and recorded**.

## 7. Organization from a Dispute Perspective

### 7.1 The issue is not “phenomena in general,” but “appropriation of the observation-document structure”

If a dispute arises, the issue should be organized as follows.

- The mere fact that another person observed a similar phenomenon is not the issue.
- The mere fact that someone referred to a phenomenon common in reality is also not the issue.
- The issue is whether the cutting method, classification method, connection method, transition structure, and definition structure established by a specific observation document were effectively taken and used.

In other words, the object of dispute is not **the use of phenomena in general**, but **whether the observation-document structure was appropriated**.

### 7.2 Axis loss as a whole is better understood as an “observation package” than as an individual name

When axis loss is considered as a whole, it is not a matter of one single word or one single definition.

Rather, it is closer to a package composed of the following.

- A higher-level observation coordinate
- Subordinate observation units
- Boundaries and exclusion criteria
- Transition and intersection tags
- Relationship-explanation structure
- Document-level fixation method for improving re-observability

Therefore, from a dispute perspective as well, it is more coherent to understand axis loss as an **observation-document package** rather than as **a single concept**.

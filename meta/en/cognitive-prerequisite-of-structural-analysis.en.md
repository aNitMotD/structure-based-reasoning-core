# Meta Record on the Cognitive Prerequisites of Structural Analysis

## 0. Purpose

This document is a meta record for organizing the view that **structural analysis is not merely a procedure, but an analytical act with cognitive prerequisites**.

This document does not claim that any specific scholar or researcher made the same claim as the author’s view.  
Rather, it serves the following purposes:

- to record adjacent research terrain in which cognition and structure have been treated together;
- to organize shareable sequences and conditions at the entry level of general structural analysis;
- to distinguish points where structural analysis may be mistaken for form, procedure, or relational description;
- to confirm that the author’s view is located at the intersection of several adjacent lines rather than belonging to a single existing field;
- to record the problem that, when cognition is removed from structural analysis, the analysis is reduced to a checklist or representational artifact.

---

## 1. Central Proposition

The central proposition of this document is as follows.

> Structural analysis is not the performance of a fixed procedure, but a procedure of cognitively detecting, separating, and reconstructing the object, elements, relations, boundaries, transitions, patterns, and exceptions.

Accordingly, cognition is not an additional element in structural analysis.  
Cognition is an **operating condition** required for structural analysis to come into being.

Procedures may be necessary for transmission and education.  
However, the moment a procedure replaces cognitive judgment, structural analysis loses its original meaning and only its external form remains.

In other words, the problem of structural analysis is not simply the following.

```text
Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 → Result
```

The more fundamental problem is the following.

```text
What should be fixed as the object?
What should be regarded as elements?
Where should the boundary be placed?
Which relation should be regarded as the core relation?
Under what condition does the state change?
Which repetition should be regarded as the same structure?
Which exception changes the structure itself?
```

A procedure without these judgments is not structural analysis, but closer to a procedural listing that imitates the form of structural analysis.

## 3. Adjacent Research Terrain 1: Lines That Treat Cognition and Structure Together

### 3.1 Jean Piaget — Cognitive Structure and Self-Regulating Structure

Piaget treated cognitive development not as a mere accumulation of behavior, but as a process of schema organization, accommodation, and equilibration.  
His work is also connected to a line that explains structure in terms of wholeness, transformation, and self-regulation.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- cognition is structurally organized;
- structure is not merely a sum of elements, but has wholeness, transformation, and self-regulation;
- structure is not separable from the way humans understand.

Differences:

- Piaget’s focus is developmental psychology and the process of knowledge construction.
- The author’s focus is the cognitive operating conditions required for structural analysis to come into being.

### 3.2 Claude Lévi-Strauss — Unconscious Structures Behind Cultural Phenomena

Lévi-Strauss attempted to analyze the universal or unconscious structures operating behind myths, kinship, and cultural phenomena.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- the same underlying structure may exist even when the surfaces differ;
- repeated relational structures are examined rather than individual cases alone;
- human cognitive classification systems may operate as a background for structural analysis.

Differences:

- Lévi-Strauss’s focus is the structural analysis of culture, myth, and kinship.
- The author’s focus is domain-independent structural analysis procedures and the cognitive fixation of boundaries, relations, and transitions.

### 3.3 Discourse Analysis, Cognitive Linguistics, and Text Comprehension Research

Discourse analysis and cognitive linguistics treat language structure, discourse structure, and text structure as factors that affect human understanding and information processing.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- structure is not separable from cognitive processing;
- surface text and actual comprehension structure are distinguished;
- coherence, relationality, and context construction can become central problems of analysis.

Differences:

- These lines mainly focus on language comprehension and discourse processing.
- The author’s focus is the cognitive prerequisites of general structural analysis, not limited to language.

## 4. Adjacent Research Terrain 2: Tacit Knowledge, Expertise, and Cognition in Practice

### 4.1 Michael Polanyi — Tacit Knowledge

Polanyi argued that humans know more than they can explicitly state.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- an analyst may not be able to immediately articulate all the criteria they use for judgment;
- structural detection ability cannot be fully reduced to propositional knowledge or procedural knowledge;
- what appears to be a “sense” or “intuition” may in fact be accumulated cognitive identification ability.

Differences:

- Polanyi emphasizes the existence and character of tacit knowledge.
- The author’s view is closer to separating and recording the cognitive operating part as a condition for structural analysis, rather than leaving tacit knowledge as it is.

### 4.2 The Dreyfus Brothers — Skill Acquisition and Expert Intuition

The Dreyfus brothers’ model of skill acquisition holds that beginners rely on rules and procedures, but as skill develops, they come to grasp the whole situation and judge intuitively.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- procedures are necessary at the novice stage;
- expert-level analysis, however, does not come into being through procedural performance alone;
- excessive fixation on rules and procedures can weaken the ability to see the situation as a whole.

Differences:

- The Dreyfus line addresses skill and expertise development.
- The author’s view more directly addresses the problem that the cognitive operating part is lost when structural analysis is reduced to procedure.

### 4.3 Donald Schön — Reflection in Practice

Schön argued that experts in real situations do not simply apply theories or procedures, but frame problems within situations and adjust while observing the results of action.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- analysis is not the application of a fixed procedure, but cognitive operation within a situation;
- a problem is not simply given, but reframed within the situation;
- the analyst re-establishes structure while interacting with the object.

Differences:

- Schön focuses on professional practice and reflective practice.
- The author’s view centers on the cognitive conditions of structural analysis itself and the problem of procedural reduction.

## 5. Adjacent Research Terrain 3: Pattern Recognition, Deep Structure, and Expert Representation

### 5.1 Herbert Simon / Gary Klein — Intuition as Pattern Recognition

The Simon and Klein line of naturalistic decision-making research understands intuition not as a mysterious sense, but as experience-based pattern recognition.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- intuition is not arbitrary emotion or guessing;
- structural cues can be recognized quickly through repeated experience;
- problem-solving ability may be closer to the ability to read situational cues and structural patterns than to mere compliance with procedure.

Differences:

- This line focuses on decision-making and field judgment.
- The author’s view treats object fixation, boundary-setting, and transition checking before judgment as conditions for structural analysis to come into being.

### 5.2 Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser — Surface Features and Deep Structure

The expert-novice research of Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser is connected to the point that experts classify problems not by surface context or keywords, but by underlying principles or deep structure.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- if the structure is the same, context may not be the core variable of structural analysis;
- novices see surface features, while experts see deep structure;
- the core of problem-solving ability lies in how a problem is represented structurally.

Differences:

- This research line mainly starts from physics problem-solving and expertise research.
- The author’s view treats the cognitive procedure of general structural analysis beyond any specific domain.

## 6. Adjacent Research Terrain 4: Relational Structure Mapping, Systems Thinking, and Boundary Judgment

### 6.1 Dedre Gentner — Structure-Mapping Theory

Gentner’s structure-mapping theory explains analogy not as a simple comparison of surface similarity, but as a process of mapping the relational structure of one domain onto another domain.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- what matters is not surface similarity between objects, but correspondence between relational structures;
- different domains may share the same relational structure;
- structural analysis may prioritize relations, relations among relations, and systematic correspondence over domain content.

Differences:

- Gentner’s focus is analogy and relational structure mapping.
- The author’s view includes not only relational mapping, but also object fixation, boundaries, transitions, exceptions, and reconstruction within one’s own context.

### 6.2 Peter Checkland — Soft Systems Methodology

Checkland’s Soft Systems Methodology focuses not on immediately solving well-defined problems, but on learning and structuring what the problem is within complex and ambiguous problem situations.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- a problem may not be clearly given from the beginning;
- before solution-making, the problem situation must be cognitively recognized and structured;
- in complex situations, “what is the problem?” itself becomes the object of analysis.

Differences:

- SSM belongs to a systems-thinking line with a methodological form.
- From the author’s view, even when a methodological form exists, structural analysis weakens if that form replaces the cognitive operating part.

### 6.3 Werner Ulrich — Boundary Critique / Critical Systems Heuristics

Ulrich’s Critical Systems Heuristics treats boundary judgment—what is included and excluded in systems analysis—as important.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- analysis begins not only by looking inside the object, but by determining how far the object extends;
- boundary judgment is central to structural analysis;
- boundary-setting can lead to questions of responsibility and attribution of judgment.

Differences:

- Ulrich addresses boundary judgment in critical systems thinking and social practice.
- The author’s view absorbs this as one cognitive condition of general structural analysis.

## 7. Synthetic Position

When the lines above are grouped together, the following adjacent terrain is formed.

```text
Piaget
= cognitive structure and self-regulating structure

Lévi-Strauss
= unconscious relational structures behind surface cultural phenomena

Discourse / cognitive linguistics
= language/text structure and cognitive processing

Polanyi
= tacit knowledge that cannot be fully explained in words

Dreyfus
= novices rely on rules, while experts intuitively grasp the whole situation

Schön
= reflective expertise that frames and adjusts problems within practice

Simon / Klein
= intuition as experience-based pattern recognition

Chi / Feltovich / Glaser
= experts classify problems by deep structure rather than surface features

Gentner
= mapping relational structures across domains

Checkland
= structuring problem situations before solution-making

Ulrich
= boundary judgment as the starting point of analysis
```

Each of these partially addresses the following elements:

- cognitive structure;
- unconscious relational structure;
- language structure and understanding;
- tacit knowledge;
- intuition;
- expertise;
- pattern recognition;
- deep structure;
- relational mapping;
- problem situation structuring;
- boundary judgment.

However, lines that directly integrate all of these into the following proposition are rare.

> Structural analysis is not a procedure, but a professional cognitive procedure that separates deep structure from surface context, cognitively fixes the object, elements, relations, boundaries, transitions, patterns, and exceptions, and then reconstructs them within one’s own context.

## 9. Separation Between the General Entry Point of Structural Analysis and Specific Formalization

The eight steps of general structural analysis organized in this document are a shareable entry point.

However, the following exceed the scope of this document:

- the specific formalization method of a particular observation system;
- higher-level coordinate design;
- the definition and fixation method of lower-level observation units;
- special tagging of transition points and intersecting structures;
- the design of responsibility, judgment, and attribution structures inside a specific methodology;
- separate supplemental axes that include the cognitive formation effects after formalization.

In other words, the entry point of general structural analysis can be publicly disclosed and shared.  
However, the entire specifically formalized structural package does not immediately become identical to the public entry point.

## 12. Compressed Statements

> Cognition is not the object of structural analysis, but the operating condition required for structural analysis to come into being. Procedures enable transmission, but the moment a procedure replaces cognitive judgment, structural analysis is reduced to a checklist.

> The general entry point of structural analysis may be organized as object fixation, element separation, relation identification, boundary-setting, transition checking, pattern extraction, exception checking, and structural summary. However, this sequence itself does not guarantee structural analysis. The core is the ability to cognitively fix what is to be regarded as structure at each step.

> Surface is the result, and structure is the mode of combination that makes that result possible. Structural analysis is not the description of a surface, but closer to reading backward the hidden boundaries and transition points.

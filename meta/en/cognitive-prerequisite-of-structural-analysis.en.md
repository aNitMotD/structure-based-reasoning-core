# Meta Record on Cognition-Conditioned Structural Analysis

## 0. Purpose

This document is a meta record for organizing the view that **structural analysis is not merely a procedure, but an analytical act with cognitive conditions of formation**.

This document does not claim that any specific scholar or researcher made the same claim as the author’s view.  
Rather, it serves the following purposes:

- to record adjacent research terrain in which cognition and structure have been treated together;
- to organize shareable sequences and conditions at the entry level of general structural analysis;
- to distinguish points where structural analysis may be mistaken for form, procedure, or relational description;
- to confirm that the author’s view is located at the intersection of several adjacent lines rather than belonging to a single existing field;
- to record the problem that, when cognition is removed from structural analysis, the analysis is reduced to a checklist or representational artifact.

This document is not a full exposition of each theory.  
However, it does not intentionally omit representative core concepts required to identify each figure or line of thought.

Accordingly, the organizing standard of this document is as follows.

```text
This document does not provide a detailed exposition of each entire theory.
However, it explicitly states representative concepts whose omission would distort the figure or line being discussed.
```

---

## 1. Central Proposition

The central proposition of this document is as follows.

> Structural analysis is not the performance of a fixed procedure, but a procedure of cognitively detecting, separating, and reconstructing the object, elements, relations, boundaries, transitions, patterns, and exceptions.

Accordingly, cognition is not an additional element in structural analysis.  
Cognition is an **operating condition** required for structural analysis to come into being.

Procedures may be necessary for transmission and education.  
However, the moment a procedure replaces cognitive judgment, structural analysis loses its original meaning and only its external form remains.

In other words, the problem of structural analysis is not simply the following.

```text
Step 1 → Step 2 → Step 3 → Result
```

The more fundamental problem is the following.

```text
What should be fixed as the object?
What should be regarded as elements?
Where should the boundary be placed?
Which relation should be regarded as the core relation?
Under what condition does the state change?
Which repetition should be regarded as the same structure?
Which exception changes the structure itself?
```

A procedure without these judgments is not structural analysis, but closer to a procedural listing that imitates the form of structural analysis.

## 3. Adjacent Research Terrain 1: Lines That Treat Cognition and Structure Together

### 3.1 Jean Piaget — Cognitive Structure, Assimilation, Accommodation, Equilibration, and Self-Regulating Structure

Representative core concepts:

- schema
- assimilation
- accommodation
- adaptation
- equilibration
- disequilibrium
- wholeness
- transformation
- self-regulation

Piaget treated cognitive development not as a mere accumulation of behavior, but as a process of schema organization and transformation.  
A schema is a cognitive frame through which experience is interpreted and organized.

Assimilation is the operation of taking a new object into an existing schema and interpreting it through that schema.  
Accommodation is the operation of modifying the schema itself when an object cannot be explained by the existing schema.  
Adaptation can be understood as the process of adjusting the relation between the environment and cognitive structure through assimilation and accommodation.

Equilibration is the process by which cognitive structure is stabilized again while the imbalance between assimilation and accommodation is adjusted.  
Disequilibrium occurs when an existing schema does not fit a new object or situation, and this mismatch becomes a trigger for accommodation and reconstruction.

Piaget’s work is also connected to a line that explains structure in terms of wholeness, transformation, and self-regulation.  
Structure is not understood as a mere sum of elements, but as a way in which elements relate within a whole, undergo transformations, and maintain a certain order by themselves.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- cognition is structurally organized;
- there is an operation of assimilation through which a new object is interpreted within an existing structure;
- when an existing structure cannot explain the object, there is an operation of accommodation through which the structure itself is adjusted;
- mismatch or disequilibrium can become a trigger for the reconstruction of cognitive structure;
- structure is not merely a sum of elements, but has wholeness, transformation, and self-regulation;
- structure is not separable from the way humans understand.

Differences:

- Piaget’s focus is developmental psychology and the process of knowledge construction.
- The author’s focus is the cognitive operating conditions required for structural analysis to come into being.
- From the author’s view, assimilation and accommodation are connected less to the explanation of developmental stages and more to the cognitive branch point at which an analyst either reads an object through an existing structure or adjusts the structure-recognition frame itself.

### 3.2 Claude Lévi-Strauss — Unconscious Structure, Binary Opposition, and Transformation

Representative core concepts:

- structuralism
- unconscious structure
- binary opposition
- mytheme
- surface narrative and deep structure
- transformation
- relational structure
- cultural classification system

Lévi-Strauss attempted to analyze the universal or unconscious structures operating behind myths, kinship, and cultural phenomena.  
What matters in his work is not merely the surface content of individual stories, but the relational structures that recur across stories and cultural phenomena.

In myth analysis, he did not treat myths simply as plots or collections of symbols.  
Rather, he attempted to understand them through smaller relational units called mythemes and through their arrangement.  
He also analyzed binary oppositions that frequently appear in cultures and myths, in order to see how stories that differ on the surface may share similar deep relations.

Transformation is an important concept in the Lévi-Straussian line.  
Different myths or cultural phenomena may appear to be separate cases, but they may also be the result of one relational structure being transformed into another form.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- the same underlying structure may exist even when the surfaces differ;
- repeated relational structures are examined rather than individual cases alone;
- relational units and oppositional structures are examined rather than surface narratives alone;
- structural correspondences among multiple transformations are examined rather than a single case alone;
- human cognitive classification systems may operate as a background for structural analysis.

Differences:

- Lévi-Strauss’s focus is the structural analysis of culture, myth, and kinship.
- The author’s focus is domain-independent structural analysis procedures and the cognitive fixation of boundaries, relations, and transitions.
- The author’s view centers less on revealing the deep structure of particular cultural phenomena and more on how structures are fixed and separated across diverse problem situations.

### 3.3 Discourse Analysis, Cognitive Linguistics, and Text Comprehension Research

Representative core concepts:

- discourse structure
- cohesion
- coherence
- frame
- schema
- conceptual metaphor
- conceptual metonymy
- mental spaces
- conceptual blending
- situation model
- context construction

Discourse analysis and cognitive linguistics treat language structure, discourse structure, and text structure as factors that affect human understanding and information processing.  
In these lines, what matters is not only the meaning of a single sentence, but how multiple sentences and contexts connect to form an overall structure of understanding.

Discourse analysis treats text or utterance not as a mere sequence of sentences, but as a flow of meaning organized through cohesion and coherence.  
Cognitive linguistics treats language understanding as connected to the activation of conceptual structures through frames, schemas, conceptual metaphors, and conceptual metonymies.

The lines concerning mental spaces and conceptual blending explain that multiple conceptual spaces may be constructed and combined in the process of understanding.  
The situation model is connected to the view that a reader constructs not merely the surface sentences of a text, but the situation structure to which those sentences refer.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- structure is not separable from cognitive processing;
- surface text and actual comprehension structure are distinguished;
- frames and schemas organize the structure of understanding;
- coherence, relationality, and context construction can become central problems of analysis;
- the relational structure that makes a textual surface possible may matter more than the textual surface itself.

Differences:

- These lines mainly focus on language comprehension and discourse processing.
- In these lines, coherence, frames, and context construction are primarily used to explain how texts or utterances form understandable flows of meaning.
- The author’s focus is the cognitive prerequisites of general structural analysis, not limited to language.
- From the author’s view, text structure may be one application area of structural analysis, but structural analysis itself is not limited to text analysis.
- The author’s view focuses less on discourse coherence or text comprehension structures themselves, and more on the conditions under which language, context, and constraints operate in boundary and transition judgments about actual objects of analysis.

## 4. Adjacent Research Terrain 2: Tacit Knowledge, Expertise, and Cognition in Practice

### 4.1 Michael Polanyi — Tacit Knowledge, Focal Awareness, and Subsidiary Awareness

Representative core concepts:

- tacit knowledge
- explicit knowledge
- focal awareness
- subsidiary awareness
- from-to structure
- indwelling
- integrative awareness
- emergent formation of meaning

Polanyi argued that humans know more than they can explicitly state.  
Tacit knowledge does not merely mean knowledge that is difficult to explain in words.  
It is connected to the view that, when we understand an object, we rely on clues and embodied structures of judgment that are not directly focalized.

One important distinction in Polanyi’s work is the distinction between focal awareness and subsidiary awareness.  
Focal awareness is awareness of the object to which we directly attend, while subsidiary awareness consists of the clues, sensations, habits, and technical skills we rely on in the background in order to understand that object.

The from-to structure refers to an awareness structure that moves from subsidiary clues toward a focal object.  
Indwelling is connected to the state of entering into a tool, language, technique, or system of judgment and seeing the world through it.
In this sense, meaning is formed emergently through the integration of subsidiary cues toward a focal object, rather than being reducible to the sum of isolated elements.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- an analyst may not be able to immediately articulate all the criteria they use for judgment;
- structural detection ability cannot be fully reduced to propositional knowledge or procedural knowledge;
- subsidiary clues that the analyst does not directly focalize may contribute to the detection of the whole structure;
- when subsidiary clues are separated and focalized, their meaning within the original structure may change;
- what appears to be a “sense” or “intuition” may in fact be accumulated cognitive identification ability.

Differences:

- Polanyi emphasizes the existence and character of tacit knowledge.
- The author’s view is closer to separating and recording the cognitive operating part as a condition for structural analysis, rather than leaving tacit knowledge as it is.
- The author’s view attempts, within limits, to verbalize the conditions under which structural detection operates tacitly, rather than turning tacit knowledge itself into authority.

### 4.2 The Dreyfus Brothers — Skill Acquisition and Expert Intuition

Representative core concepts:

- skill acquisition model
- novice
- advanced beginner
- competent
- proficient
- expert
- rule dependence
- situational judgment
- embodied expertise
- expert intuition

The Dreyfus brothers’ model of skill acquisition holds that beginners rely on rules and procedures, but as skill develops, they come to grasp the whole situation and judge intuitively.  
This model is generally described through the stages of novice, advanced beginner, competent, proficient, and expert.

A novice relies on rules that are independent of context.  
As skill develops, the person sees situational cues, priorities, exceptions, and the whole context together rather than merely applying simple rules.  
An expert may operate by quickly identifying the important structure in the whole situation, rather than calculating every judgment through explicit rules.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- procedures are necessary at the novice stage;
- expert-level analysis, however, does not come into being through procedural performance alone;
- excessive fixation on rules and procedures can weaken the ability to see the situation as a whole;
- structural analysis ability can be strengthened through staged skill development and accumulated experience in situational identification;
- expert intuition may be not arbitrary guessing, but the ability to rapidly classify situational structure.

Differences:

- The Dreyfus line addresses skill and expertise development.
- The author’s view more directly addresses the problem that the cognitive operating part is lost when structural analysis is reduced to procedure.
- The author’s view focuses less on skill stages themselves and more on object fixation, boundary-setting, and transition checking at the moment structural analysis comes into being.

### 4.3 Donald Schön — Reflection in Practice, Problem Setting, and Reframing

Representative core concepts:

- technical rationality
- knowing-in-action
- reflection-in-action
- reflection-on-action
- problem setting
- framing
- reframing
- reflective practitioner

Schön argued that experts in real situations do not simply apply theories or procedures, but frame problems within situations and adjust while observing the results of action.  
The technical rationality he criticized is connected to a view that treats problems as already well-defined and solves them by applying theoretical knowledge.

Knowing-in-action refers to the tacit knowing that an expert already possesses and performs within action.  
Reflection-in-action is the operation of revising judgment while action is still ongoing, in response to how the situation reacts.  
Reflection-on-action is the operation of looking back after action and reconstructing the meaning of the process.

What matters for Schön is not only problem solving, but also problem setting.  
The professional does not merely solve a given problem, but adjusts in practice what the problem is, from which frame it should be seen, and how an existing frame should be changed.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- analysis is not the application of a fixed procedure, but cognitive operation within a situation;
- a problem is not simply given, but reframed within the situation;
- before problem solving, how the problem itself is set is important;
- the analyst re-establishes structure while interacting with the object;
- reflection-in-action and reframing can be connected to the process of resetting boundaries and relations in structural analysis.

Differences:

- Schön focuses on professional practice and reflective practice.
- The author’s view centers on the cognitive conditions of structural analysis itself and the problem of procedural reduction.
- From the author’s view, reflection is not treated simply as a general feature of practice, but as part of an analytical situation in which structural fixation and boundary resetting are required.

## 5. Adjacent Research Terrain 3: Pattern Recognition, Deep Structure, and Expert Representation

### 5.1 Herbert Simon — Bounded Rationality, Heuristics, and Expert Intuition

Representative core concepts:

- bounded rationality
- heuristic
- problem space
- search
- satisficing
- chunking
- expert memory
- intuition as pattern recognition

The Simon line does not treat human judgment merely as complete calculation or optimization.  
Humans judge under limited time, information, and computational capacity, and heuristics and problem-space search play important roles under these conditions.

Expert intuition can be understood not as a mysterious sense, but as a rapid recognition of patterns accumulated through experience.  
An expert can recognize meaningful clusters of cues and structures through repeated experience, rather than calculating each individual cue separately.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- intuition is not arbitrary emotion or guessing;
- structural cues can be recognized quickly through repeated experience;
- under limited resource conditions, identifying structural cues may matter more than complete calculation;
- problem-solving ability may be closer to the ability to read situational cues and structural patterns than to mere compliance with procedure.

Differences:

- The Simon line focuses on decision-making, problem solving, bounded rationality, and cognitive models.
- The author’s view treats object fixation, boundary-setting, and transition checking before judgment as conditions for structural analysis to come into being.
- From the author’s view, intuition is not a ground that justifies a conclusion, but is limited to an operation that rapidly detects structural cues.

### 5.2 Gary Klein — Naturalistic Decision-Making and Recognition-Primed Decision

Representative core concepts:

- naturalistic decision-making
- Recognition-Primed Decision
- situational cue
- cue recognition
- mental simulation
- time pressure
- field expert judgment
- sensemaking

Klein’s naturalistic decision-making research addresses how experts in real fields make judgments under limited time and incomplete information.  
The Recognition-Primed Decision model holds that experts do not compare and calculate all possible alternatives, but recognize familiar patterns from situational cues and then examine a possible action through mental simulation.

From this view, rapid judgment is not necessarily impulsive judgment.  
It is an operation that combines pattern recognition accumulated through field experience, interpretation of situational cues, and internal simulation of expected results.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- field judgment may not assume complete information and long analysis time;
- fast expert judgment may rely on situational cues and pattern recognition;
- before judgment, it is important to identify which cues should be treated as structurally meaningful;
- mental simulation can be connected to structural thinking that reviews possible paths and results in advance.

Differences:

- The Klein line focuses on field decision-making and action selection.
- The author’s view focuses more on identifying problem structure, boundaries, transitions, and attribution of responsibility than on action selection.
- From the author’s view, rapid judgment is not the goal; it is meaningful only in a limited sense when structural identification has been sufficiently performed.

### 5.3 Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser — Surface Features and Deep Structure

Representative core concepts:

- expert-novice comparison
- problem representation
- surface feature
- deep structure
- underlying principle
- principle-based categorization
- knowledge organization
- problem classification

The expert-novice research of Chi, Feltovich, and Glaser is connected to the point that experts classify problems not by surface context or keywords, but by underlying principles or deep structure.  
A novice may be drawn to surface features such as objects, situations, words, or scenes that are visible on the surface.  
An expert represents the problem by the principles, relations, constraints, and structures that make the problem possible.

What matters here is not only what answer is used to solve the problem.  
The more fundamental difference lies in what structure the problem is represented as, what category it is placed into, and what principle is invoked.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- if the structure is the same, context may not be the core variable of structural analysis;
- novices see surface features, while experts see deep structure;
- the core of problem-solving ability lies in how a problem is represented structurally;
- structural analysis is not merely classifying surface cases as they appear, but closer to reading backward the principles and relations that made the case possible.

Differences:

- This research line mainly starts from physics problem-solving and expertise research.
- The author’s view treats the cognitive procedure of general structural analysis beyond any specific domain.
- From the author’s view, deep structure may include not only problem-solving principles, but also boundaries, transitions, exceptions, and attribution of responsibility.

## 6. Adjacent Research Terrain 4: Relational Structure Mapping, Systems Thinking, and Boundary Judgment

### 6.1 Dedre Gentner — Structure-Mapping Theory and Systematicity

Representative core concepts:

- Structure-Mapping Theory
- base domain
- target domain
- relational alignment
- structural alignment
- surface similarity
- structural similarity
- one-to-one correspondence
- systematicity principle
- higher-order relations
- candidate inference

Gentner’s structure-mapping theory explains analogy not as a simple comparison of surface similarity, but as a process of mapping the relational structure of one domain onto another domain.  
What matters is not how similar the surface properties of objects are, but how relations correspond.

In this theory, the relational structure of a base domain is mapped onto a target domain.  
Relational alignment, consistency among relations, and one-to-one correspondence matter more than similarity of isolated attributes.

The systematicity principle is the view that a connected system of relations produces a stronger analogical correspondence than a single isolated relation.  
In particular, a relational structure that includes higher-order relations can have stronger explanatory force than simple similarity.

Candidate inference is connected to the process of generating a possible inference that a relation holding in one domain may also hold in another domain.  
Here, inference is not an arbitrary extension based on surface similarity, but is constrained by correspondence between relational structures.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- what matters is not surface similarity between objects, but correspondence between relational structures;
- different domains may share the same relational structure;
- structural analysis may prioritize relations, relations among relations, and systematic correspondence over domain content;
- a network of relations and higher-order relations may matter more than a single relation;
- structural correspondence requires constrained mapping conditions rather than arbitrary metaphor.

Differences:

- Gentner’s focus is analogy and relational structure mapping.
- The author’s view includes not only relational mapping, but also object fixation, boundaries, transitions, exceptions, and reconstruction within one’s own context.
- From the author’s view, mapping may be part of structural analysis, but structural analysis as a whole is not reducible to analogy theory.

### 6.2 Peter Checkland — Soft Systems Methodology and Structuring Problem Situations

Representative core concepts:

- Soft Systems Methodology
- problem situation
- rich picture
- root definition
- CATWOE
- human activity system
- conceptual model
- purposeful activity
- accommodation
- learning system

Checkland’s Soft Systems Methodology focuses not on immediately solving well-defined problems, but on learning and structuring what the problem is within complex and ambiguous problem situations.  
SSM does not assume from the beginning that a problem is a single fixed object.  
Rather, it represents the problem situation from multiple viewpoints, reveals relevant actors, worldviews, transformation processes, and constraints, and models possible activity systems.

A rich picture is a way of representing stakeholders, conflicts, flows, constraints, and differences in perspective together, rather than immediately fixing a problem situation through a textual definition.  
A root definition is a statement that defines, from a particular viewpoint, what human activity system is to be treated as the object of analysis.  
CATWOE is used as a device for checking the root definition through customers, actors, transformation, worldview, owners, and environmental constraints.

A human activity system is a concept for modeling a system in which people carry out purposeful activities.  
A conceptual model is not a model that simply copies reality, but one that expresses how meaningful activities should be connected according to a particular root definition.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- a problem may not be clearly given from the beginning;
- before solution-making, the problem situation must be cognitively recognized and structured;
- in complex situations, “what is the problem?” itself becomes the object of analysis;
- viewpoints, actors, constraints, and environmental conditions can become part of the problem structure;
- problem definition and boundary-setting may be not additional steps of analysis, but starting conditions.

Differences:

- SSM belongs to a systems-thinking line with a methodological form.
- From the author’s view, even when a methodological form exists, structural analysis weakens if that form replaces the cognitive operating part.
- The author’s view does not follow the SSM procedure itself, but checks how problem-situation structuring touches the cognitive conditions of structural analysis.

### 6.3 Werner Ulrich — Boundary Critique / Critical Systems Heuristics

Representative core concepts:

- boundary judgment
- boundary critique
- Critical Systems Heuristics
- reference system
- sources of motivation
- sources of control
- sources of knowledge
- sources of legitimacy
- is questions
- ought questions
- affected stakeholders
- justification

Ulrich’s Critical Systems Heuristics treats boundary judgment—what is included and excluded in systems analysis—as important.  
Boundary judgment is not merely a question of how far to set the scope of analysis.  
It includes what is regarded as the problem, whose purpose is used as the standard, who has control, what knowledge is recognized, and who grants legitimacy.

CSH treats boundary judgment by asking about the sources of motivation, control, knowledge, and legitimacy.  
It also distinguishes is questions, which ask whether things are actually arranged in a certain way, from ought questions, which ask whether they should be arranged that way.

This approach requires the analyst to reveal the reference system that they already assume.  
Which stakeholders are included, which stakeholders are excluded, and who is affected but not included in the decision process also become objects of boundary critique.

Points of contact with the author’s view:

- analysis begins not only by looking inside the object, but by determining how far the object extends;
- boundary judgment is central to structural analysis;
- not only what is included, but also who has purpose, control, knowledge, and legitimacy affects the boundary of analysis;
- boundary-setting can lead to questions of responsibility and attribution of judgment;
- invisible assumptions and excluded stakeholders can change the structure of the analytical result.

Differences:

- Ulrich addresses boundary judgment in critical systems thinking and social practice.
- The author’s view absorbs this as one cognitive condition of general structural analysis.
- From the author’s view, boundary judgment is not limited to questions of social legitimacy, but can also arise in technical, operational, linguistic, and responsibility structures.

- the specific formalization method of a particular observation system;
- higher-level coordinate design;

However, the entire specifically formalized structural package does not immediately become identical to the public entry point.

## 12. Compressed Statements

> Cognition is not the object of structural analysis, but the operating condition required for structural analysis to come into being. Procedures enable transmission, but the moment a procedure replaces cognitive judgment, structural analysis is reduced to a checklist.

> The general entry point of structural analysis may be organized as object fixation, element separation, relation identification, boundary-setting, transition checking, pattern extraction, exception checking, and structural summary. However, this sequence itself does not guarantee structural analysis. The core is the ability to cognitively fix what is to be regarded as structure at each step.

> Surface is the result, and structure is the mode of combination that makes that result possible. Structural analysis is not the description of a surface, but closer to reading backward the hidden boundaries and transition points.

> Adjacent research terrain provides multiple concepts that touch structural analysis, but the existence of those concepts alone does not establish identity with the author’s formalization. Identity can be examined separately only when specific materials and comparison targets are presented.

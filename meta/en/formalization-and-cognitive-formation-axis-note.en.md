# Observation Note on Formalization and the Cognitive Formation Axis

## 1. Purpose

This document is a separate observation note for recording **what occurs after the formalization of axis loss and its subordinate observation units has been read**.

The focus of this document is not the definition or boundary setting of the formalization itself, but a separate layer consisting of the following.

- The effect of formalization on reader cognition
- Changes in interpretive state after cognitive formation
- Changes in cost structure
- Changes in path selection
- Weakening of post-event narrative and defense structures

Therefore, this document is not an internal definition document for axis loss, CIF, TIF, or SIF. It is a supplemental document addressing the **cognitive layer after exposure to formalization**.

---

## 2. Basic Proposition

The basic proposition of this document is as follows.

> Formalization forms cognition.

Cognition here does not mean simple acquisition of information.  
It does not mean merely coming to know a few words. It means the **formation of a cognitive structure** in which the very way one interprets the world, actions, and procedures changes afterward.

In other words, after a formalization is read, a different interpretive state is formed.

- Structures that were previously unseen begin to become visible.
- What previously appeared to be simple description begins to be read as structural difference.
- Boundaries that were previously undifferentiated begin to be distinguished.

---

## 3. Meaning of Cognitive Formation

### 3.1 Formalization creates structural “visibility”

The core change that occurs after reading a formalization is a **change in visibility**.

For example, the following begin to become visible.

- The separation between constraint recognition and constraint inheritance
- The asymmetry between R/F paths
- The difference between phenomena in general and formalized structures
- The possibility of mismatch between procedural record and actual performance
- The difference between structural identity and mere expressive modification

The core of this change is not that one learns one additional new fact.  
Rather, a structure that was previously unseen transitions into a state in which it is **now visible**.

### 3.2 Cognitive formation is above the layer of action

Action occurs on top of cognition.

- Procedural performance occurs on top of cognition.
- Structural distinction is possible on top of cognition.
- Responsibility attribution cannot be separated from cognitive state.
- Post-event narrative is also constructed on top of cognition.

Therefore, cognitive formation is not a single element parallel to action.  
It is closer to a **higher-level prerequisite layer** that enables or limits action.

---

## 4. Irreversibility of Cognitive Formation

### 4.1 Meaning of irreversibility

Irreversibility in this document does not mean physical impossibility of deletion.  
The core point is as follows.

- After reading a formalization, one cannot return to the state before reading it.
- After a structure begins to become visible, it becomes difficult to maintain the previous state in which it was not visible.
- It becomes difficult to treat the formed interpretive conditions as if they had never existed.

In other words, irreversibility is closer to **structural irreversibility of cognitive state**.

### 4.2 Irreversibility is more accurately understood as a change in cost structure

If irreversibility is described simply as “once you know it, you cannot forget it,” the expression can easily become overstated.  
A more accurate expression is as follows.

> Formalization forms cognition, and formed cognition changes the cost structure.

In other words, after reading a formalization, the cost of maintaining the same interpretation or denial as before changes.  
It is this change in cost structure that makes return to the previous state difficult.

---

## 5. Change in Cost Structure

### 5.1 Difference before and after cognitive formation

Before cognitive formation, the cost of maintaining a certain objection or narrative may be low.

For example:

- I did not know.
- I had never seen the structure.
- I arrived at it independently.
- It is merely a similar phenomenon.
- There is no problem because the procedure was followed.

However, after reading the formalization and forming cognition, additional cost is required to maintain the same narrative.

- One must ignore a structure that is now visible.
- One must deny boundaries that are already distinguishable.
- One must explain structural similarity.
- One must maintain one’s own narrative in conflict with the formed cognition.

In other words, cognitive formation changes the cost of maintaining post-event narratives.

### 5.2 Change in cost structure affects path selection

Formed cognition does not end as a simple change in awareness.  
Because it changes the cost structure, it also affects subsequent path selection.

- Even if maintaining the F path was low-cost before,
- after cognitive formation, the cost of maintaining F may increase;
- after a certain point, the R path may become the lower-cost path.

Therefore, cognitive formation ultimately causes **changes in path selection**.

---

## 6. Relationship to Claims of Independent Arrival

After exposure to formalization, the claim that one “independently arrived at the same structure” is structurally weakened.

The reason is that the condition for independent arrival usually presupposes the following.

> Did the person arrive at the structure without having previously seen it?

However, if a person has already read the formalization and cognition has been formed, it becomes difficult to describe a later identical or similar structure as pure independent arrival.  
In other words, the point of this document is not to prohibit someone’s thought itself, but that **the conditions for sustaining an independent-arrival narrative are weakened**.

---

## 7. Relationship to the “I Did Not Know” Defense

After cognitive formation, the “I did not know” defense is also weakened.

The reason can be organized into two layers.

### 7.1 The person has already entered a state in which the structure can be seen

After reading the formalization, structures that were previously unseen begin to become visible.  
In this state, it becomes increasingly difficult to maintain complete ignorance.

### 7.2 It conflicts with reasonable cognitive accessibility

If a document is public and accessible, and the relevant formalization is connected to structurally similar work,  
a narrative such as “I did not see it” or “I did not know” may conflict with reasonable cognitive accessibility.

Therefore, the core point in this document is not punishment or prohibition, but that **the cost of maintaining a post-event denial narrative increases**.

---

## 8. Relationship Between Procedure and Cognition

The point that formalization forms cognition is also connected to procedural issues.

Procedure belongs to the layer of action.  
However, the truth value of a procedure is not complete without cognition.

For example, a “yes” on a checklist can diverge as follows.

- Without cognition, it may be merely a sign.
- With cognition, it may become a record of actual performance.

In other words, even if the same action marker is used, its truth value changes depending on cognitive state.  
In this sense, procedure is not a higher-level substitute for cognition. Rather, it is closer to a lower-level layer whose meaning is determined by cognition.

---

## 9. Position as the Fourth Axis

If the entire prior-notice system is understood as having four axes, the cognitive formation axis becomes the fourth axis.

1. Freedom of action
2. Fixation of responsibility
3. Distinction of structure
4. Formation of cognition

The fourth axis is not simply parallel to the preceding three axes.  
Rather, it is closer to a **higher-level prerequisite axis** that prevents the preceding three axes from being neutralized by post-event denial or independent-arrival narratives.

That is:

- without cognition, structural distinction is weakened;
- without cognition, responsibility attribution becomes loose; and
- without cognition, post-action evaluation is weakened.

Therefore, the cognitive formation axis can be understood as the most fundamental prerequisite axis among the four axes.

---

## 10. Operating Chain

The argument of this document can be compressed as follows.

> Formalization forms cognition.  
> Formed cognition changes the cost structure.  
> The changed cost structure changes path selection.  
> The changed path selection changes outcomes and post-event narratives.

This chain is the core operating structure that occurs at the cognitive layer after exposure to formalization.

---

## 11. Summary

The core points of this document are as follows.

1. Formalization is not simple information transmission. It may cause the formation of a cognitive structure.
2. Cognitive formation is a state change that makes previously unseen structures visible.
3. This state change belongs to a higher-level prerequisite layer above the layer of action.
4. The irreversibility of cognitive formation is better understood as a change in cost structure than as physical impossibility of deletion.
5. Formed cognition increases the cost of post-event narratives, maintenance of the F path, independent-arrival claims, and the “I did not know” defense.
6. Therefore, cognitive formation operates as a structural factor that changes path selection and outcomes.
7. In this sense, the cognitive formation axis is a separate supplemental axis that operates above freedom of action, fixation of responsibility, and distinction of structure.

---

## 12. One-Line Summary

> Formalization forms cognition; formed cognition changes the cost structure; and the changed cost structure changes subsequent path selection and post-event narratives.

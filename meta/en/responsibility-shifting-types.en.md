# Seven Types of Responsibility Shifting

## Overview

The seven types below may appear in different forms, but they share a common structure: an attempt to attribute an externally generated act, result, or effect back to the original observer.  
The difference lies in the entry point of the attack. The common feature is that each type attempts to collapse the separation between observation and application, record and execution, influence and responsibility.

> The types presented in this document are not an exhaustive list of all possible responsibility-shifting types.  
> They summarize only the types that have been observed or structurally identified so far.  
> In actual cases, a single type may not appear in isolation; two or more types may be combined, overlapped, or composed into a more complex responsibility-shifting structure.

---

## Type 1. Toolization-Based Shifting

### Definition

A type in which an observational structure is taken and turned into an external product, system, service, or feature, and when a failure occurs as a result, responsibility is shifted back to the original author.

### Core Structure

- An observational structure is taken.
- It is converted into an externally executable tool or function.
- A failure or damage occurs.
- Responsibility for the result is shifted back to the observer.

### Shifting Method

**Use-after-failure shifting**

### Core Blocking Axis

Toolization and use are external acts, and responsibility for their results belongs to the party that toolized or used the structure.

---

## Type 2. Copy-and-Modification Shifting

### Definition

A type in which notices or structures are copied, edited, or rearranged and attached to another context, after which problems in the modified result are connected back to the original author.

### Core Structure

- Sentences, structures, or declarations from the original text are copied.
- They are modified to fit another party's purpose.
- The modified output causes a problem.
- Responsibility for that problem is shifted back to the original author.

### Shifting Method

**Post-copy responsibility shifting**

### Core Blocking Axis

A copied or modified output is an external artifact separate from the original text, and responsibility for its results belongs to the party that copied or modified it.

---

## Type 3. Existence-Based Shifting

### Definition

A type in which, even without directly using the repository content, a party claims that the repository's public status, license, accumulated references, or mere existence caused disadvantage to its business or position.

### Core Structure

- Nothing is taken.
- Nothing is directly applied.
- Nevertheless, the party claims that market disadvantage arose from the existence of the public observation and the lawful exercise of rights.
- That disadvantage is attributed back to the observer.

### Shifting Method

**Cumulative-effect shifting from lawful acts**

### Core Blocking Axis

The indirect effects of lawful publication, license selection, or exercise of rights do not automatically create a duty to compensate.

---

## Type 4. Accuracy-Abuse Shifting

### Definition

A type in which a party understands the observational structure, intentionally reproduces or abuses it, and after damage occurs, shifts responsibility to the observer's accuracy by claiming that the abuse was possible because the observation was accurate.

### Core Structure

- The observational structure is read and understood.
- The structure is intentionally abused or reproduced.
- Damage occurs.
- The possibility of abuse is reconstructed as a consequence of the observation's accuracy.
- Responsibility is shifted back to the observer.

### Shifting Method

**Accuracy-based shifting**

### Core Blocking Axis

Accuracy may explain the possibility of an act, but it does not transfer responsibility for intentional abuse to the observer.

---

## Type 5. Internal Attack on Observational Responsibility

### Definition

A type in which an explicit statement that observational responsibility is retained is used as the basis for attacking the observer, by demanding responsibility for matters not observed or for omissions outside the defined scope of observation.

### Core Structure

- The phrase "observational responsibility is retained" is seized upon.
- Unobserved areas or elements outside the defined scope are raised as issues.
- The party argues, "Why did you not observe this as well?"
- The scope of observation is pushed toward unlimited expansion.

### Shifting Method

**Attribution attempt based on alleged omission in observation scope**

### Core Blocking Axis

Observational responsibility is responsibility for the coherence of what was observed. It is not responsibility for the existence of what was not observed, nor for an unlimited domain context.

---

## Type 6. Third-Party Citation Path Shifting

### Definition

A type in which a third party, such as a journalist, researcher, external analyst, or market participant, legitimately cites or analyzes an observation, and when reputational, market, or enterprise-value damage occurs as a result, the starting point of the indirect causal chain is shifted back to the observer.

### Core Structure

- The observer does not directly deal with the allegedly harmed party.
- A third party cites or analyzes the observation.
- The third party's analysis influences the market, institutions, or readers.
- Damage occurs.
- That damage is attributed back to the initial observer.

### Shifting Method

**Third-party-mediated causal shifting**

### Core Blocking Axis

Citation, reporting, analysis, and market reaction are independent acts of the respective third parties, and responsibility for their results belongs to those actors.

---

## Type 7. Declaration-Reality Gap Attack

### Definition

A type in which, because external citations, judicial references, or factual influence have increased over time, a party argues that the original declarations of non-authority, non-instruction, or time-boundedness are no longer true, thereby attacking the truthfulness of the notice itself.

### Core Structure

- The initial declaration is acknowledged as having been true.
- The party claims that external influence has increased over time.
- The party argues that the declaration and present reality no longer match.
- This is reconstructed as a basis for defective representation or invalidation of the declaration.

### Shifting Method

**Attack on the truthfulness of the declaration**

### Core Blocking Axis

Functional declarations and factual influence are separate axes. The formation of factual authority outside the document does not automatically make the document's internal functional declaration false.

# Structural Expansion Order

## Types 1-2

These are the simplest forms.  
A party takes something, uses it, fails, and then attempts to return that failure to the original author.

## Types 3-4

These move beyond the axis of direct use and target existence itself or accuracy itself.

## Type 5

This is the first type that uses the internal wording of the notice itself as a basis for attacking observational responsibility.

## Type 6

Even if the allegedly harmed party did not directly use the repository, this type attempts to connect the damage through third-party citation and market reaction.

## Type 7

Rather than directly asserting responsibility attribution, this type attacks the premise under which the notice operates: whether the declaration and reality still correspond.

---

# Common Structure

Although these seven types appear different, they structurally share the following features.

1. Some external act or reaction occurs.
2. Damage, disadvantage, pressure, or change appears as a result.
3. That result is reconnected back to the original observer.
4. The notice does not block the act itself; instead, it blocks shifting by fixing the line of responsibility attribution for the result.

In other words, the common pattern is:

> An attempt to attribute an external result back to the internal observer.

# Overall Summary

The seven types of responsibility shifting are distinct attack vectors through which responsibility for externally generated results is attributed back to the observer, even though the observer did not directly execute or operate the relevant act. These vectors include use, modification, market effects, abuse, alleged omission in observation scope, third-party citation effects, and declaration-reality gap attacks.

The difference lies in the entry point of the attack. The common feature is that all of them attempt to return the result of an external act to the observer.  
The notice framework does not prevent the act itself; rather, it blocks responsibility shifting by fixing the line of responsibility attribution for the resulting outcome.
